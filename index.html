Tech Partners workforce brief

Designing Oklahoma’s data center workforce advantage.

Energy abundance, large-scale AI infrastructure, and expanding campus development across Tulsa, Muskogee County, Pryor, and Stillwater create a rare opening for Oklahoma to build a durable talent pipeline across trades, digital operations, and higher education.

Why now

Meta has broken ground on a Tulsa AI-optimized campus with more than $1 billion in projected regional investment, while Google has announced a broader Oklahoma expansion measured in the billions and tied to cloud and AI infrastructure.

The opening

Oklahoma has the ingredients that hyperscale development values: land, power, logistics, industrial know-how, and a cost profile that can compete with larger metros.

Winning the next decade will depend less on whether projects are announced and more on whether Oklahoma can supply electricians, controls specialists, fiber technicians, data center operators, cyber talent, and advanced analytics leaders at scale.

Three-tier framework

A durable workforce plan should treat construction, operations, and innovation as connected pipelines rather than isolated programs.

Trades

Build and maintain the physical plant

  • High-voltage electrical, substations, backup power, and mission-critical safety.
  • Advanced HVAC and cooling systems designed for dense compute loads.
  • Fiber, structured cabling, controls, and commissioning support.
Technology

Operate infrastructure with uptime discipline

  • Rack-and-stack, hardware lifecycle support, and server administration.
  • Network operations, monitoring, incident response, and physical-digital security.
  • Bridge pathways for adjacent talent from utilities, oil and gas, telecom, and SCADA.
Higher education

Lead analytics, power strategy, and innovation

  • AI, machine learning, data engineering, and big data analytics.
  • Power systems, sustainable infrastructure, and grid integration.
  • Cybersecurity, governance, policy, and leadership development.

Eight actions for Oklahoma

  1. Launch a statewide data center workforce council led jointly by industry, CareerTech, higher ed, and workforce agencies.
  2. Stand up regional pre-apprenticeship and fast-track bootcamp models near Tulsa, Pryor, Muskogee, and Stillwater.
  3. Create bridge programs for oil and gas, telecom, utility, and industrial maintenance talent.
  4. Publish clear career maps with certifications, wages, and advancement ladders.
  5. Embed employer-led labs and professors of practice into community college and university programs.
  6. Use relocation and return-to-Oklahoma incentives for hard-to-fill technical roles.
  7. Coordinate internships, co-ops, and apprenticeships across all three tiers.
  8. Measure success with completions, placements, wage growth, and retention in Oklahoma.
